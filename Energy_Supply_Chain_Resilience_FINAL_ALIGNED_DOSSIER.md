## PART 1 — THE REAL CRISIS THAT MAKES THIS PROBLEM STATEMENT LIVE, NOT HYPOTHETICAL

### 1.1 Condensed timeline (full detail in your working notes; keep this version for the pitch deck)

- **28 Feb 2026** — US and Israel strike Iran; Iran's IRGC closes the Strait of Hormuz within 48 hours.
- **2–4 Mar 2026** — Strait restricted to Iran-approved vessels only; QatarEnergy declares force majeure on LNG exports after strikes on Ras Laffan.
- **Mar–Apr 2026** — Brent spikes intraday to a reported high around $116–119/barrel (multiple trackers cite slightly different peak figures — present as "over $115" rather than a single precise number unless you cite one specific source); combined Gulf oil production loss reaches an estimated 6.7–10 million barrels/day at peak; both Hormuz and the Red Sea/Bab el-Mandeb corridor are disrupted simultaneously for the first time in modern shipping history.
- **8 Apr / 11–12 Apr 2026** — Temporary ceasefire, then collapse of Islamabad talks; US imposes a naval blockade on Iran-bound/outbound shipping.
- **17–18 Jun 2026** — MOU signed to end the war and reopen the strait; blockade lifted.
- **8–13 Jul 2026** — Renewed clashes; US strikes Iranian targets again after an attack on a Cyprus-flagged vessel; Brent moves back up into the high $70s–low $80s range; **the situation remains genuinely live as you build for this hackathon.**

### 1.2 What actually happened to Brent crude (use dated figures, not a single static number)
- WTI closed 2025 at $57.35/barrel; Brent closed 2025 at $60.92/barrel (a ~19–20% decline over calendar 2025, i.e., the market was *soft* right before the war started).
- Intraday spike to a reported ~$115–119/barrel on/around 9 March 2026.
- Retreated to the $90s by early June as the ceasefire held, then fell further toward $70–73 by late June.
- Renewed spike into the high $70s/low $80s range in mid-July following the 8–13 July re-escalation.
- **Takeaway for your Scenario Modeller:** this is not a single price shock — it's a volatile, multi-peaked event with at least three distinct escalation/de-escalation cycles so far. A good scenario model should represent *volatility regime changes*, not just "price went up once."

## PART 2 — INDIA'S ACTUAL RESPONSE (this is your ground-truth validation dataset)

- India's crude imports fell an estimated **~23% in March 2026** (≈81 million barrels in the first half of March vs. ≈105 million barrels in the equivalent February period).
- Non-Hormuz sourcing share rose from **~55% to ~70%** within weeks, drawing on West Africa, the Americas, and Russia.
- The government invoked the **Essential Commodities Act** to issue an **LPG Control Order within 8 days** of the closure, since India imports ~60% of its LPG and historically ~90% of that transited Hormuz; domestic LPG output rose an estimated ~25% (some trackers cite a rise from ~35,000 to ~54,000 tonnes/day within a week).
- A **24×7 control room** was stood up by the Ministry of Petroleum & Natural Gas to monitor stocks and fuel availability nationally.
- Macroeconomic fallout: current account deficit widened from roughly $95B to $120B over the year (largely the oil import bill); the rupee depreciated ~4.9% to around ₹93/dollar; RBI reportedly spent ~$46B smoothing (not defending) the decline; oil marketing companies' daily under-recovery reportedly reached ~₹2,400 crore at one point; the excise-duty freeze is estimated to have cost the exchequer close to ₹1.1 lakh crore (~$12B).
- Despite this, full-year GDP growth still came in around 7.7%, and retail fuel prices rose only ~7% domestically against a global average closer to 25–30% — a genuinely strong empirical resilience case.
- **India's Petroleum Minister Hardeep Singh Puri** publicly reassured markets that diversification was underway — a real, quotable, on-record government statement you can reference (search his name + "Strait of Hormuz 2026" for the specific press statement if you want a direct citation for your deck).

This section is your single most valuable evidence base: it's the exact "response speed and quality" ground truth the brief's evaluation criteria (disruption signal detection lead time, procurement alternative quality, end-to-end response time) are implicitly asking you to benchmark against.

### 3.2 Major refiners/refineries (public, well-documented — use as your digital twin's core processing nodes)

- **Bharat Petroleum (BPCL)** — Mumbai, Kochi, Bina (Madhya Pradesh, via BORL)
- **Hindustan Petroleum (HPCL)** — Mumbai, Visakhapatnam

For a hackathon-scale digital twin, you don't need proprietary throughput telemetry (which isn't public) — you need the **node list with public capacity figures** (available in each company's investor-facing annual reports and in PPAC's public refinery capacity tables) plus a clearly-labeled *simulated* utilization layer driven by your scenario modeller. ### 3.4 Pipeline network (domestic distribution layer)
IOCL, BPCL, HPCL and GAIL between them operate India's major crude and product pipeline network connecting coastal refineries/import terminals to inland refining and distribution hubs (e.g., Salaya–Mathura crude pipeline, Kandla–Bhatinda, Vizag–Vijayawada, Mumbai–Manmad–Delhi product pipelines, and GAIL's natural gas trunk pipelines like HBJ — Hazira-Bijaipur-Jagdishpur). Exact current capacity/utilization figures are best sourced fresh from each company's latest annual report or the Ministry of Petroleum's Basic Statistics on Indian Petroleum & Natural Gas publication (PPAC publishes this annually and it's public) — pull the latest edition rather than relying on older cached figures, since pipeline capacity expands over time.

## PART 4 — THE FIVE SUB-SYSTEMS, DEEPLY RESEARCHED AGAINST THE ACTUAL BRIEF TEXT

### 4.1 Geopolitical Risk Intelligence Agent
*Brief's spec: "ingests news feeds, shipping AIS data, sanctions registries, and commodity price signals to produce a live supply disruption probability score by corridor and supplier — updated continuously, not weekly."*

- **News feeds:** GDELT 2.0/GKG/DOC 2.0 API — free, ~15-minute refresh, CAMEO-coded events with a Goldstein intensity score and a Global Knowledge Graph tagging themes including `ECON_OILPRICE`. This is your best free option for continuous (not weekly) signal ingestion.
- **Shipping AIS data:** AISstream.io (free WebSocket tier, bounding-box subscription, MMSI/message-type filtering) is your most practical option for a hackathon timeline; subscribe specifically to the Strait of Hormuz box (~24–28°N, 54–58°E) plus India's main receiving ports (Jamnagar/Sikka, Vadinar) so your "corridor" scoring is literally India-route-specific, not generic.
- **Sanctions registries:** OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list is public and machine-readable (CSV/XML, updated regularly) — directly relevant given the brief's own mention of "renewed US sanctions pressure on Iranian oil exports." The 2026 crisis included a real, dated example you can cite: the US Treasury revoked a 60-day sanctions waiver on Iranian oil in early July 2026, effective 17 July 2026 — a concrete, recent, checkable sanctions event.
- **Commodity price signals:** EIA Open Data API (free) for crude import/price/stock data; publicly available Brent/WTI daily price feeds for the headline number.
- **Corridor/supplier-specific scoring:** don't just produce one national risk number — score it per corridor (Hormuz vs. non-Hormuz routes) and per major supplier country to India (Iraq, Saudi Arabia, UAE, US, Russia, etc.) so the "probability score by corridor and supplier" language in the brief is answered literally, not approximately.

### 4.2 Disruption Scenario Modeller
*Brief's spec: "simulates specific events — Hormuz partial closure, OPEC+ emergency cut, Red Sea shipping suspension — and computes cascading impacts on refinery run rates, domestic fuel prices, power sector stress, and GDP trajectory under each scenario."*

Build exactly the three named scenarios, using real precedent data for each so your model isn't purely speculative:
1. **Hormuz partial closure** — you have the entire live 2026 case as ground truth (Part 1–2 above). This should be your primary, most-detailed, most-validated scenario.
2. **OPEC+ emergency cut/production change** — OPEC+ is a real, trackable body; its ministerial meeting schedule and quota announcements are public (search "OPEC+ production decision 2026" for the latest at build time — this changes monthly, so pull current data rather than relying on this dossier's snapshot). Model this as a *supply-shrinkage* scenario distinct from a *transit-blockage* scenario — the cascading effects differ (a production cut changes global available barrels; a transit blockage strands existing barrels behind a chokepoint) and demonstrating you understand that distinction is a technical-excellence signal.
3. **Red Sea shipping suspension** — the ongoing Houthi campaign against Red Sea/Bab el-Mandeb shipping since late 2023 is a real, well-documented, *slower-burn* disruption type (as opposed to Hormuz's sudden-shock type) — useful to show your modeller handles both disruption speeds, not just one.

For the "cascading impacts" chain, model it explicitly as: corridor disruption → crude import volume/cost change → refinery input mix change (grade compatibility constraints — different crude grades suit different refinery configurations, a real technical constraint worth representing even simply) → domestic fuel price pass-through (India's actual OMC under-recovery and excise-duty-freeze mechanism from Part 2 is your real precedent for this step) → power-sector stress (India's gas-fired generation share, LPG availability) → GDP/macro effects (current account deficit, rupee, RBI intervention — again, Part 2 gives you real 2026 figures to calibrate against).

### 4.3 Adaptive Procurement Orchestrator
*Brief's spec: "identifies and ranks alternative crude sources and logistics routes, factoring in spot market pricing, tanker availability, port congestion, and refinery grade compatibility — generating procurement recommendations that procurement teams can act on within hours."*

- **Alternative crude sources:** India's real 2026 diversification pattern (Part 2) — West Africa, the Americas (including a documented pivot toward US and Guyanese crude in recent years), and Russia — gives you a real, validated "correct answer" set to benchmark your recommender's output against. If your system, given the same starting conditions, recommends a similar diversification mix to what India's refiners actually executed, that's a genuine, demonstrable accuracy claim for your pitch — far stronger than an unfalsifiable "94% accurate" claim.
- **Spot market pricing:** EIA and public Brent/WTI feeds, plus regional grade differentials (Dubai/Oman crude benchmark is the standard reference for Gulf grades relevant to Asian refiners — worth including alongside Brent).
- **Tanker availability / port congestion:** AISstream.io-derived vessel counts and port-arrival/departure timestamps at India's major crude ports (Jamnagar/Sikka, Vadinar, Paradip) can approximate a congestion signal without needing a paid maritime-intelligence subscription.
- **Refinery grade compatibility:** each refinery is configured for a range of crude "sweetness"/API gravity; representing even a simplified compatibility matrix (e.g., which of your Part 3.2 refineries can process heavier/sourer alternative grades vs. only light/sweet Gulf grades) is a real technical-depth signal that most competing teams are unlikely to bother modeling.
- **"Within hours" requirement:** this is a UX/latency claim you should literally demo live — show the system going from a simulated disruption trigger to a ranked recommendation output in front of the judges, with a visible timer.

### 4.4 Strategic Reserve Optimisation Agent
*Brief's spec: "models optimal SPR drawdown schedules against supply gap forecasts, refinery demand curves, and replenishment window estimates — providing decision support to policymakers under time pressure."*

- Use the **real, verified** ISPRL numbers from Part 0: 5.33 MMT total capacity, ~9.5 days cover at full capacity, ~64% actual fill level as of the March 2026 RTI disclosure, plus the 64.5-day OMC commercial buffer (74 days total national cover) — this gives your optimisation agent a real starting state to model drawdown scenarios from, rather than an assumed/synthetic starting inventory.
- Model against the IEA's 90-day recommended benchmark explicitly — showing "here's the gap between India's actual cover and the international benchmark, and here's how a coordinated drawdown-plus-import-diversification strategy could bridge it faster than either lever alone" is a genuinely useful, demonstrable piece of decision support, and it's the kind of insight a real policymaker would find credible.
- IEA's own coordinated emergency stock release mechanism (member countries collectively releasing reserves during a supply crisis — reportedly around 400 million barrels released globally at the peak of the 2026 crisis, per the earlier v1/v2 research) is a real international-coordination precedent worth referencing, even though India isn't a full IEA member (only observer status) — it's relevant context for "what a fully-optimized global response looks like" that your India-specific agent can be benchmarked against.

### 4.5 Supply Chain Digital Twin
*Brief's spec: "Geospatial simulation of India's full energy supply network — from wellhead to refinery to distribution — that enables continuous 'what-if' analysis and serves as a persistent intelligence platform."*

- Node set: use Part 3 in full (SPR caverns, refineries, ports, pipeline network) — this is the literal "wellhead to refinery to distribution" chain the brief names.
- Academic grounding: Ivanov, Dolgui & Sokolov's (2019) "Digital Supply Chain Twins: Managing the Ripple Effect" is the standard foundational reference for the "digital twin" concept in supply chain contexts — cite it if you use the term, so a technically literate judge sees you're grounded in real literature.
- Architecture pattern: knowledge-graph + graph neural network approaches (e.g., GraphSAGE-style risk propagation across a heterogeneous supplier/route/refinery graph) are a documented, buildable pattern directly matching the brief's own "Knowledge Graphs (supplier-route-risk-refinery relationships)" suggested technology — you don't need to train custom GNN weights under hackathon time pressure; even a NetworkX-based risk-propagation model over your real node/edge data would demonstrate the right architecture pattern.
- "Continuous what-if analysis" — this is your interactive demo centerpiece: let a judge pick a scenario (or adjust a slider — e.g., "% of Hormuz capacity available") and watch the graph visibly re-route/re-score in real time.
